Hitting the Boundary: First-Passage & Absorption

The unbounded walker gives you diffusion (the √N law). The bounded walker — the one that dies at the edge — gives you a completely different and equally beautiful theory. The punchline: the same random walk that produces diffusion turns out to solve classical physics equations (Laplace’s and Poisson’s) once you put walls around it.

The one object behind everything: the discrete Laplacian

Every result here comes from a single, almost trivial observation. Suppose \(f(x,y)\) is some quantity defined at each cell, and the walker computes it by averaging over where it might step next. Because each of the 4 directions is equally likely:

$$f(x,y) = \tfrac{1}{4}\big[f(x{+}1,y) + f(x{-}1,y) + f(x,y{+}1) + f(x,y{-}1)\big]$$

“A cell’s value equals the average of its 4 neighbors.” That’s the discrete mean-value property, and a function satisfying it is called harmonic. The continuous version is Laplace’s equation \(\nabla^2 f = 0\) — the equation that governs steady-state heat, electrostatic potential, soap films, ideal fluid flow. The random walk is a little machine for solving it. Two different questions about the walk plug into this machine.

Question 1 — Which wall do you hit? (Laplace’s equation)

Ask: starting from cell \((x,y)\), what’s the probability \(h(x,y)\) that the walker eventually exits through, say, the top edge rather than any other?

Condition on the first step. With probability ¼ each you move to a neighbor, and from there the probability is just \(h\) at that neighbor. So:

$$h(x,y) = \tfrac14\big[h(x{+}1,y)+h(x{-}1,y)+h(x,y{+}1)+h(x,y{-}1)\big]$$

That’s the mean-value property again — \(h\) is harmonic. The boundary conditions write themselves: \(h = 1\) on the top edge (you’ve arrived) and \(h = 0\) on the other edges. So “which wall does a random walker hit?” is exactly the same math as “what’s the steady-state temperature, if the top wall is held at 1° and the others at 0°?” This probability distribution over exit points has a name — harmonic measure — and it’s a deep object in analysis.

Question 2 — How long until you escape? (Poisson’s equation)

Now the question the Avg stat is measuring. Let \(T(x,y)\) = expected number of steps to fall off the board, starting at \((x,y)\). Condition on the first step again — but this time you spent a step to take it, so there’s a “+1”:

$$T(x,y) = 1 + \tfrac14\big[T(x{+}1,y)+T(x{-}1,y)+T(x,y{+}1)+T(x,y{-}1)\big]$$

Rearrange, and the averaging-minus-itself becomes the discrete Laplacian:

$$\tfrac14\big[\textstyle\sum \text{neighbors}\big] - T = -1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \nabla^2 T = -4$$

with \(T = 0\) on the boundary. That’s Poisson’s equation with a constant source — the equation for the temperature of a plate with uniform internal heating, or the deflection of a stretched membrane under uniform pressure, or the stress in a twisted beam. The expected escape time of the dying walker is literally the same function as the sag of a drumhead.

The clean 1D case you can hold in your hand

In 2D those PDEs need numerics, but the 1D version (a walker on \(\{0,1,\dots,N\}\) absorbed at either end — the classic Gambler’s Ruin) has gorgeous closed forms:

  • Probability of exiting at the right end, starting from \(k\): \(\;\dfrac{k}{N}\;\) — perfectly linear (the 1D harmonic function is just a straight line).
  • Expected time to absorption, starting from \(k\): \(\;k(N-k)\).

From the center \(k = N/2\), that expected time is \(N^2/4\) — it grows like the square of the box size.

The thread that ties it back to diffusion

That \(N^2\) scaling is not a coincidence — it’s the √N law wearing a different hat. The free walker covers distance \(\sim\sqrt{\text{steps}}\), so to cross a box of width \(L\) it needs \(\sim L^2\) steps. Bounded and unbounded are two views of one fact:

$$\text{distance} \sim \sqrt{\text{time}} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \text{time-to-escape} \sim (\text{size})^2$$

You can test this live in the game. The expected walk length on the Recenter board should scale like (grid size)². So if you roughly double the Grid slider, the Avg walk length should roughly quadruple. Try 41 vs 81 and watch the Avg stat — that quadratic jump is Poisson’s equation showing through the simulation.
