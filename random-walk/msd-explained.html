The Classical Unbounded Walker & Mean-Squared Displacement

This is the “textbook” random walk — the one before we added walls — and it has one beautiful, clean result that our bounded version doesn’t.

The setup: an unbounded walker

Imagine the same walker — start at the origin, each step go U/D/L/R with equal probability — but on an infinite grid. No edges, it never falls off, it just wanders forever. The question physicists and mathematicians care about is:

After N steps, how far from home is it?

Your gut might say “well, it takes a step every time, so after N steps it’s roughly N cells away.” That’s wrong, and why it’s wrong is the whole point.

Why distance doesn’t grow like N

The walker is as likely to step back toward home as away from it. The steps partly cancel. So the average displacement — the average of the position vector itself — is exactly zero: it’s equally likely to drift any direction, so on average it goes nowhere.

$$\langle \vec{r}_N \rangle = 0$$

But that’s clearly not the useful answer — the walker obviously does get farther away over time, it just doesn’t have a preferred direction. The fix is to measure the squared distance, which can’t cancel (squares are always positive):

$$\langle r_N^2 \rangle = \langle x_N^2 + y_N^2 \rangle$$

This is the mean-squared displacement (MSD) — averaged over many independent walks.

The clean result

Here’s the famous law. For this simple walk:

$$\boxed{\langle r_N^2 \rangle = N}$$

The mean-squared displacement grows linearly with the number of steps. Let me show you why, because the derivation is short and it’s the satisfying part.

Write the position after N steps as a sum of individual step vectors:

$$\vec{r}_N = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec{s}_i$$

Then:

$$r_N^2 = \vec{r}_N \cdot \vec{r}_N = \sum_{i} \vec{s}_i \cdot \vec{s}_i + \sum_{i \neq j} \vec{s}_i \cdot \vec{s}_j$$

Now take the average of both sums:

  • First sum (the diagonal terms): each step is length 1, so \(\vec{s}_i \cdot \vec{s}_i = 1\). There are N of them → contributes N.
  • Second sum (the cross terms): here’s the key. Different steps are independent, and each step averages to zero direction. So \(\langle \vec{s}_i \cdot \vec{s}_j \rangle = \langle \vec{s}_i \rangle \cdot \langle \vec{s}_j \rangle = 0\) for \(i \neq j\). Every cross term vanishes.

What survives is just N. That independence — the cross terms canceling — is the entire mechanism.

What it actually means

The headline consequence: take the square root to get a typical distance.

$$r_{\text{typical}} \sim \sqrt{N}$$

Distance grows like the square root of steps, not steps. Concretely:

Steps (N)Typical distance from home
100~10 cells
10,000~100 cells
1,000,000~1,000 cells

To get twice as far, the walker needs four times as many steps. It explores, but agonizingly slowly — it keeps wandering back over old ground. This √N law is the signature of diffusion, and it’s not a coincidence that it shows up everywhere: ink spreading in water, heat flowing through metal, a pollutant dispersing in air, stock-price models. They’re all the same random walk in disguise. The diffusion equation you may have seen in physics is literally the continuous limit of this process, and that’s where “\(\langle r^2 \rangle \propto t\)” comes from.

Why our game can’t show this

Two reasons our bounded walker breaks the clean law:

  1. Absorbing boundary. Our walk ends when it hits the edge — it gets killed, not reflected. So long walks (the ones that would wander far) are systematically cut short. We never observe the large-N behavior because those walks don’t survive.
  2. Finite box. Even ignoring death, the walker can’t get farther than the grid’s half-width, so \(\langle r^2 \rangle\) would plateau rather than grow linearly forever.

So if I plotted MSD-vs-N for our current game and drew the “\(\langle r^2 \rangle = N\)” line next to it, they’d diverge fast and it’d look like the theory is broken — when really it’s just a different problem (first-passage / absorption, which has its own elegant theory about exit times and which wall you hit).
