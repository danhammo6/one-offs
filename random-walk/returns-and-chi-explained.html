Returns to Center & Why χ² Drifts

Two questions from watching the simulation run: what does “returns to center” mean, and why does χ² keep climbing instead of settling down?

Part 1 — “Returns to center”

“Returns to center” counts how many times the walker steps back onto its exact starting cell after leaving it — averaged over all the walks so far.

What it’s measuring

On the Recenter board, every walk begins at the center cell. As the walker wanders, it sometimes finds its way back to that origin cell. Each time it lands there again, that’s one “return.” The stat is:

$$\text{Returns to center} = \frac{\text{total returns across all walks}}{\text{number of walks}}$$

So if it reads 4.2, the walker has revisited its starting square an average of 4.2 times per walk before falling off the edge.

In the code it’s literally this check on each step (index.html):

if (bx === cx && by === cy) centerReturns += 1;

where (cx, cy) is the start cell. (Note: the very first placement at the start isn’t counted as a “return” — only subsequent visits are.)

Why it’s an interesting statistic

This is a window into a genuinely deep result — Pólya’s recurrence theorem (1921):

A simple random walk in 1D and 2D is recurrent — it returns to its starting point infinitely often, with probability 1. In 3D and higher it is transient — there’s a real chance it wanders off and never comes back.

The folklore phrasing (attributed to Kakutani) is:

“A drunk man will find his way home, but a drunk bird may get lost forever.”

A drunkard stumbling on a 2D street grid is certain to eventually stagger back to their starting lamppost. A drunk bird flying in 3D might not.

The catch in our game

Here’s the subtle part: Pólya’s theorem says “infinitely often” — but that’s for a walk on an infinite, unbounded plane that runs forever. Our Recenter board is finite and the walk dies when it hits the edge. So in practice the count is cut short: the walker either keeps returning to center for a while, or escapes off the board and the walk ends.

So our “returns to center” doesn’t reach infinity — it’s the finite number of returns before absorption at the boundary. What you’ll notice:

  • Larger grid → the edge is farther away → walks last longer → more returns to center on average. (The unbounded ideal, recurrence, is the limit as the grid → ∞.)
  • Smaller grid → the walker escapes quickly → few returns.

It’s the same recurrent-vs-transient story you saw with the MSD plot, viewed from a different angle: in 2D the walker is “sticky” near home and keeps drifting back, which is exactly why it explores space so slowly (the √N law). The Pan board’s unbounded walker is the version where, given infinite time, that return count really would climb without bound.

Part 2 — Why χ² keeps climbing (and whether that’s a bug)

Watching the simulation, χ² appeared to creep higher and higher — reaching ~17 after half a million steps. Is that the RNG going bad? No — but it’s also not the whole story. It’s a subtle and genuinely interesting statistical effect.

χ² does not grow with N

For a fair RNG, the cumulative χ² is a stationary quantity — its expected value stays at 3 forever, no matter how many steps. It does not trend upward. Here’s why, briefly:

  • Let \(D_d = \text{count}_d - N/4\) be how far each direction’s count strays from fair.
  • As \(N\) grows, the proportions converge to 25%, but the raw deviation \(D_d\) grows like \(\sqrt{N}\) (it’s itself a random walk).
  • χ² divides those squared deviations by the expected count (\(N/4\)): the \(\sqrt{N}\) and the \(N/4\) cancel, leaving an \(O(1)\) number centered on 3.

Concretely, the statistic is

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{d=1}^{4} \frac{(\text{count}_d - N/4)^2}{N/4} = \sum_{d=1}^{4} \frac{D_d^2}{N/4}, \qquad D_d \sim \sqrt{N} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{D_d^2}{N/4} \sim O(1).$$

So a true upward divergence would only happen with a real, persistent bias (then χ² grows linearly with N and would be in the thousands by 500k steps — not 17). You’re nowhere near that.

So why does it look like it’s climbing?

This is the key insight, and it’s the flip side of the “less jumpy over time” behavior: as N grows, the cumulative χ² mixes more and more slowly.

At N=1,000, one new step noticeably moves the ratio, so it jitters fast around 3. At N=500,000, a single step barely nudges the cumulative counts — so wherever the statistic happens to be, it stays there for a long time. A chance excursion up to 17 doesn’t snap back; it lingers and reads like a trend.

It’s still stationary — given enough further steps it will wander back down toward 3 — but at half a million steps it’s moving glacially. You caught it mid-excursion on a very slow-moving value.

How to tell a real bias from slow mixing

Behavior of χ²Verdict
Jitters around 3, rare spikes above 7.81Healthy fair RNG
Drifts to ~17 and lingers at 500k stepsSlow mixing — still fair, just stuck mid-excursion
Grows roughly linearly with N (hundreds, thousands)Real, persistent directional bias
Pinned near 0 foreverSuspiciously too even — a rigged/over-regular sequence

Want to see the “fair” jitter again?

The fix that makes this visually obvious: compute χ² over a sliding window of, say, the last 10,000 moves instead of all moves since reset. A windowed χ² has a fixed mixing speed, so it keeps jittering briskly around 3 forever and never gets “stuck” at a high value. Plotting cumulative vs. windowed side by side directly demonstrates everything above.
